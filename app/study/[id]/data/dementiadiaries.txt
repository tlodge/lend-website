“As a younger person living well with one of the rare strains, we need to get the message out that there is life after diagnosis.

The label of dementia infers we are demented – we are not. We have a neurological condition, which will steal our memory, behaviours, relationships, and so much more.

Dementia comes under mental health, but the definition of each show there are very different conditions. Those of us with this condition are experts in our own conditions and have invaluable information to share to those who want to be more informed about how it is for younger people to be diagnosed with this condition.”

“Good morning everybody!

It’s Agnes, it’s Tuesday morning, it’s dull here in Scotland.

I’m just phoning to tell you again: that is another crisis averted.

I got up this morning feeling a bit hungry and a wee bit out of my routine. I decided to make my breakfast so I put my porridge on. Then I glanced at the clock and thought “Ah, you shouldn’t, it’s bed time!” So anyway, I went back to bed.

Then I thought “I’m not sleeping.” Of course I’m hungry.

I looked out and I thought “what is that noise? It must be pouring with rain.”

A little while later I thought “there’s a smell.”

And then I remembered… my porridge.

I caught it before it set off my alarm and before the fire brigade was involved. Just another day and night with Agnes, living with the diagnosis of dementia, on my own. Time to cope with the challenges that we all have to cope with. But when you are on your own, somehow or another, there is that added dimension.

I think Nancy would agree with this. Anyway, I am feeling quite upbeat and I think the reason for that was that out of the blue, from the Scottish Dementia Working Group, my friend Anne sent me a message. It was on the computer, so it was an email. Yes, I got an email message. And that is the first email message from a member of the Working Group Committee in well over a year. It just made me feel good. A wee bit of peer support goes a long way especially when it is kind of local – although she lives a bit far away. At least it is in Scotland and I don’t have to go to England or America, India and Wales.

It was nice to hear a familiar person just saying “how are ye?”

What a difference little things make to let you know that someone with dementia out there is thinking about you.

Oh, did I tell you I made the coffee and went to bed with the coffee instead of my breakfast? Anyway, I’ll finish my coffee and I’ll face the day, and I’m feeling quite chuffed with myself. Bye!”

“I want to talk about why I am trying to learn French, particularly as I was learning French about fifty-five years ago at the University of Liverpool!

When my wife and I went on holiday, a couple of years ago, to France, part of the purpose of the trip was to see if I could still speak French fluently. My old friend, Henri, was extremely generous in his welcome. We spent many happy hours talking about the world. I found it astonishingly satisfying to be able to speak French fluently again, even though I had not used the language like that since I got dementia. Henri and I have been good friends for at least sixty years and we need to continue to keep in touch.

So, when we came back, I wanted to find a teacher to keep my French going. We found a teacher, locally, who offers one-to-one French conversation. I now go once a month and she and I talk non-stop about many things. In my last lesson, she had found an article about Michelin, which was of great interest to me and Henri, as Henri used to work for them.

To start with, it wasn’t easy, but after several sessions, I began to dredge up the French that I used to know, and I can talk fluently again.

From now on, the French language is an integral part of keeping me going.”

“So, what does the phrase ‘living with dementia’ really mean, and how can this be achieved? Well, having thought about it and looked around, I’ve found that it comes in ‘can’s’. I try to keep constantly in my thoughts what I can do, and not what I can’t do anymore. Life is like riding a bicycle. All the time you’re pushing onwards, and things are fine and okay. When you stop, that’s when things fall out of balance and become difficult to control.

I watched a program recently, where they proved that it was possible to walk across custard. Due to the chemical makeup of the liquid, the harder you apply impact to its surface, the firmer it becomes. And as long as you keep on moving, you don’t sink into the mire. Dementia is like that. As long as you don’t stop, it has less chance to drag you down.

I’ve found the best way to deal with dementia is to fight back. Keep yourself as busy as possible, and get on with life with as much positivity that can be possibly mustered.

In a spirit of living with dementia, I’ve recently taken part in hover craft driving day, a segway safari date, and a tank driving day. On the latter, I got to drive four different armed vehicles, including a Chieftain tank, and a session on a quad bike. Now it was fun to see the instructor faces when I declared to them that I had dementia. I have to say, they watched my every action really carefully. I’ve also taken a helicopter ride around London. The aviation authorities were pleased to know that I was not piloting the helicopter.

Now, it’s my belief that when life throws challenges at us, we should face them head-on and fight back. I have adopted a daily mantra of ‘I may have dementia, but dementia does not have me’. In the famous words of Winston Churchill: never, never, never give up.”
